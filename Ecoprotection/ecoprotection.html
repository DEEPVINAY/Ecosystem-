ECOSYSTEM.....

About

An ecosystem is a complex community of living organisms, including plants, animals, and microbes, interacting with each other and their physical environment. Ecosystems can range in size from a small pond to a vast ocean, and can be found in a variety of terrestrial and aquatic environments. Ecosystems are characterized by the flow of energy and matter through the living and non-living components of the environment.

There are many different types of ecosystems, including forests, grasslands, wetlands, deserts, coral reefs, and freshwater ecosystems, each with its own unique set of organisms and environmental conditions. Ecosystems can be further classified based on their biomes, which are large geographical areas with similar climate and vegetation.
Ecosystems provide many important benefits to society, including the provision of food, water, and other resources, as well as the regulation of climate, disease, and natural disasters. They also provide opportunities for recreation and tourism, and have cultural and aesthetic values.However, human activities such as deforestation, overfishing, pollution, and climate change are threatening ecosystems worldwide. As a result, it is crucial to protect and restore ecosystems in order to ensure their sustainability and the continued provision of ecosystem services for human society.

There are many different approaches to protecting and restoring ecosystems, including conservation efforts, sustainable land use practices, and the restoration of degraded ecosystems. Effective ecosystem management requires interdisciplinary approaches that integrate ecological, economic, and social perspectives.

ECOLOGICAL SUCCESSION

The characteristics of population and community and also their response to environment and how such responses vary from an individual response. Let us examine another aspect of community response to environment over time. An important characteristic of all communities is that their composition and structure constantly change in response to the changing environmental conditions. This change is orderly and sequential, parallel with the changes in the physical environment. These changes lead finally to a community that is in near equilibrium with the environment and that is called a climax community. The gradual and fairly predictable change in the species composition of a given area is called ecological succession. During succession some species colonise an area and their population become more numerous whereas populations of other species decline and even disappear. The entire sequence of communities that successively change in a given area are called sere(s). The individual transitional communities are termed seral stages or seral communities. In the successive seral stages there is a change in the diversity of species of organisms, increase in the number of species and organisms as well as an increase in the total biomass. The present day communities in the world have come to be because of succession that has occurred over millions of years since life started on earth. Actually succession and evolution would have been parallel processes at that time. Succession is hence a process that starts in an area where no living organisms are therethese could be areas where no living organisms ever existed, say bare rock; or in areas that somehow, lost all the living organisms that existed there. The former is called primary succession, while the latter is termed secondary succession.

ECOSYSTEM CARBON CYCLE

When you study the composition of living organisms, carbon constitutes 49 per cent of dry weight of organisms and is next only to water. If we look at the total quantity of global carbon, we find that 71 per cent carbon is found dissolved in oceans. This oceanic reservoir regulates the amount of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. Do you know that the atmosphere only contains about 1per cent of total global carbon? Fossil fuel also represent a reservoir of carbon. Carbon cycling occurs through atmosphere, ocean and through living and dead organisms. According to one estimate 1013 kg of carbon is fixed annually in the biosphere through photosynthesis. A considerable amount of carbon returns to the atmosphere as CO2 through respiratory activities of the producers and consumers. Decomposers also contribute substantially to CO2 pool by their processing of waste materials and dead organic matter of land or oceans. Some amount of the fixed carbon is lost to sediments and removed from circulation. Burning of wood, forest fire and combustion of organic matter, fossil fuel, volcanic activity are additional sources for releasing CO2 in the atmosphere. Human activities have significantly influenced the carbon cycle. Rapid deforestation and massive burning of fossil fuel for energy and transport have significantly increased the rate of release of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere

ENERGY FLOW

Except for the deep sea hydro-thermal ecosystem, sun is the only source of energy for all ecosystems on Earth. Of the incident solar radiation less than 50 per cent of it is photosynthetically active radiation (PAR). We know that plants and photosynthetic bacteria (autotrophs), fix Sunâ€™s radiant energy to make food from simple inorganic materials. Plants capture only 2-10 per cent of the PAR and this small amount of energy sustains the entire living world. So, it is very important to know how the solar energy captured by plants flows through different organisms of an ecosystem. All organisms are dependent for their food on producers, either directly or indirectly. So you find unidirectional flow of energy from the sun to producers and then to consumers. Is this in keeping with the first law of thermodynamics? Further, ecosystems are not exempt from the Second Law of thermodynamics. They need a constant supply of energy to synthesise the molecules they require, to counteract the universal tendency toward increasing disorderliness. The green plant in the ecosystem are called producers. In a terrestrial ecosystem, major producers are herbaceous and woody plants. Likewise, producers in an aquatic ecosystem are various species like phytoplankton, algae and higher plants. You have read about the food chains and webs that exist in nature. Starting from the plants (or producers) food chains or rather webs are formed such that an animal feeds on a plant or on another animal and in turn is food for another. The chain or web is formed because of this interdependency. No energy that is trapped into an organism remains in it for ever. The energy trapped by the producer, hence, is either passed on to a consumer or the organism dies. Death of organism is the beginning of the detritus food chain/web. All animals depend on plants (directly or indirectly) for their food needs. They are hence called consumers and also heterotrophs. If they feed on the producers, the plants, they are called primary consumers, and if the animals eat other animals which in turn eat the plants (or their produce) they are called secondary consumers. Likewise, you could have tertiary consumers too. Obviously the primary consumers will be herbivores. Some common herbivores are insects, birds and mammals in terrestrial ecosystem and molluscs in aquatic ecosystem. The consumers that feed on these herbivores are carnivores, or more correctly primary carnivores (though secondary consumers). Those animals that depend on the primary carnivores for food are labelled secondary carnivores.

FOOD CHAIN

The food chain is a sequence of organisms in an ecosystem that are linked through their feeding relationships. It is the transfer of energy and nutrients from one organism to another. Typically, a food chain starts with a primary producer, such as plants, which use sunlight and nutrients from the soil to produce their own food through photosynthesis. Then, herbivores (primary consumers) consume the plants, and carnivores (secondary consumers) eat the herbivores. Finally, the top predators (tertiary consumers) consume the carnivores. Decomposers, such as fungi and bacteria, break down the dead organisms and return nutrients to the soil, which are used by the primary producers again, completing the cycle.

The Importance of Ecosystem Protection:

Ecosystem protection is essential for a variety of reasons. First, ecosystems provide many essential services to humans, such as air and water purification, pollination, and soil fertility. These services are critical for human health and well-being and are often undervalued in economic terms.
Second, ecosystems play a critical role in regulating the climate. Forests and other vegetation absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, helping to mitigate the impacts of climate change. Wetlands and other ecosystems also play an important role in sequestering carbon and reducing greenhouse gas emissions. Third, ecosystems are home to a vast array of biodiversity, from microorganisms to large mammals. Biodiversity is important for maintaining the functioning of ecosystems, providing genetic resources for agriculture and medicine, and providing aesthetic and cultural benefits.

Challenges to Ecosystem Protection:

Despite the importance of ecosystems, they are facing a range of threats from human activities. Climate change is causing changes in temperature, precipitation, and other environmental factors that are affecting ecosystems around the world. Deforestation, habitat loss, and pollution are also major threats to ecosystems, leading to the extinction of many species and the degradation of ecosystems. Additionally, the increasing demand for natural resources, such as water and minerals, is putting additional pressure on ecosystems. Land use changes, such as agricultural expansion and urbanization, are also contributing to the loss of natural habitats and the fragmentation of ecosystems.

Protecting Ecosystems:

There are many ways that we can protect ecosystems. One approach is to establish protected areas, such as national parks and wildlife reserves, where human activities are limited or prohibited. These protected areas provide important habitats for wildlife and can help to maintain biodiversity. Another approach is to promote sustainable land use practices, such as conservation agriculture, that minimize the negative impacts on ecosystems. This can include using cover crops to reduce erosion, reducing the use of pesticides and fertilizers, and rotating crops to maintain soil fertility. Restoration of degraded ecosystems is also an important approach to ecosystem protection. This can involve replanting forests, restoring wetlands, and other measures to restore degraded habitats. International cooperation is also critical for ecosystem protection. Many of the threats to ecosystems, such as climate change and habitat loss, are global in nature and require collective action to address. The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) and the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) are two examples of international agreements that aim to address these issues.

Disadvantages of Ecosystem


1. High Cost: Implementing an ecosystem protection program can be expensive, especially if it involves large-scale conservation efforts or infrastructure projects.

2. Limited Effectiveness: The effectiveness of an ecosystem protection program can be limited if it does not have sufficient resources, public support, or political will.

3. Conflict with Other Interests: Ecosystem protection efforts can sometimes conflict with other interests such as economic development, mining, or agriculture.

4. Lack of Inclusivity: An ecosystem protection program may fail to include the participation of marginalized communities, which can lead to unequal distribution of benefits.

5. Difficulty in Measuring Success: Measuring the success of an ecosystem protection program can be challenging as it involves multiple factors and long-term effects.

In conclusion, Ecosystem Protection Program has its advantages and disadvantages. However, the benefits of protecting our environment and preserving our natural ecosystems far outweigh the drawbacks. A well-planned and implemented ecosystem protection program can have a positive impact on our planet and the generations to come.

Advantages of the Ecosystem

One of the primary advantages of an ecosystem is that it supports biodiversity. By providing a range of different habitats and resources, ecosystems can sustain a diverse array of plant and animal species. This, in turn, helps to maintain a healthy balance in the ecosystem and promotes ecological resilience. Ecosystems also provide a range of essential ecosystem services, such as carbon sequestration, nutrient cycling, and water purification. These services are crucial to the health and well-being of both natural and human systems. For example, wetlands act as natural water filters, removing pollutants and impurities from water before it enters lakes, rivers, and oceans. Ecosystems also provide important economic benefits. Many industries, such as forestry, agriculture, and fisheries, rely on healthy ecosystems to produce goods and services. Additionally, ecotourism is a rapidly growing industry that provides economic benefits to local communities while also promoting conservation efforts. Overall, ecosystems provide a wide range of advantages that are essential to the health and well-being of both natural and human systems. By supporting biodiversity, providing essential ecosystem services, and generating economic benefits, ecosystems play a critical role in maintaining a healthy and sustainable planet.
